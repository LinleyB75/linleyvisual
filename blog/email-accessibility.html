Best Practices for Email Accessibility

Creating emails that everyone can read and interact with, regardless of their abilities or assistive technologies.

Email accessibility isn't just a nice-to-have feature—it's essential. When we design accessible emails, we ensure that everyone, including people using screen readers, those with visual impairments, or individuals with cognitive disabilities, can engage with our content effectively.

In this comprehensive guide, I'll walk you through the fundamental practices that every email developer should implement to create truly accessible email experiences.

Understanding Email Accessibility

Accessibility in email marketing means designing and coding emails so that all recipients can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with them. According to the World Health Organization, over 1 billion people worldwide experience some form of disability. That's a significant portion of your audience that deserves equal access to your content.

Key Principle: Accessible design benefits everyone, not just people with disabilities. Clear structure, good contrast, and readable fonts improve the experience for all users.

Semantic HTML Structure

Using proper HTML semantics is the foundation of accessible emails. Screen readers rely on the document structure to navigate content effectively.

Use Proper Heading Hierarchy

Always structure your headings logically, starting with h1 for the main title and following with h2, h3, and so on. Never skip heading levels just for visual styling.

<h1>Main Email Title</h1> <h2>Section Heading</h2> <h3>Subsection</h3> <p>Body content here</p>

Meaningful Alt Text

Every image in your email should have descriptive alt text. This text is read aloud by screen readers and displayed when images fail to load.

  • Be descriptive: Explain what the image shows, not just what it is
  • Keep it concise: Aim for 125 characters or less
  • Avoid redundancy: Don't start with "Image of..." or "Picture of..."
  • Decorative images: Use empty alt text (alt="") for purely decorative images

Color Contrast and Visual Design

Proper color contrast ensures that text is readable for people with visual impairments, color blindness, or those viewing emails in bright sunlight.

WCAG Contrast Requirements

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) recommend:

  • Normal text: Minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1
  • Large text (18pt+): Minimum contrast ratio of 3:1
  • Enhanced (AAA level): 7:1 for normal text, 4.5:1 for large text

Pro Tip: Use online tools like WebAIM's Contrast Checker to verify your color combinations meet accessibility standards.

Don't Rely on Color Alone

Never use color as the only means of conveying information. For example, if you're highlighting important text in red, also use bold formatting or an icon to ensure the meaning is clear to colorblind users.

Typography and Readability

Text formatting plays a crucial role in email accessibility. Poor typography choices can make content difficult or impossible to read.

Font Size and Line Height

  • Minimum font size: 14px for body text
  • Line height: At least 1.5 times the font size
  • Line length: Keep lines between 50-75 characters for optimal readability
  • Font choice: Use web-safe fonts or ensure fallbacks are accessible

Interactive Elements and Links

Buttons and links must be accessible to keyboard users and clearly identifiable by screen readers.

Descriptive Link Text

Avoid generic phrases like "Click here" or "Read more." Instead, use descriptive text that makes sense out of context:

  • ❌ Bad: "Click here to learn more"
  • ✅ Good: "Learn more about our accessibility features"

Touch Target Size

Ensure buttons and links are large enough to be easily tapped on mobile devices. The recommended minimum size is 44x44 pixels, with adequate spacing between interactive elements.

Table-Based Layouts

While modern web development has moved away from tables for layout, email development still relies heavily on them due to email client limitations. Here's how to make tables accessible:

  • Use role="presentation" on layout tables to tell screen readers they're not data tables
  • For data tables, include proper th headers with scope attributes
  • Add a summary or caption to explain complex tables
  • Ensure the content makes sense when linearized (read top to bottom)
<table role="presentation"> <tr> <td>Layout content here</td> </tr> </table>

Testing for Accessibility

Building accessible emails is one thing; verifying they work correctly is another. Here are essential testing methods:

Screen Reader Testing

Test your emails with actual screen readers:

  • NVDA: Free, open-source screen reader for Windows
  • JAWS: Popular commercial screen reader for Windows
  • VoiceOver: Built into macOS and iOS
  • TalkBack: Built into Android devices

Additional Testing Tools

  • Color Contrast Analyzers: WebAIM, Stark, Colour Contrast Analyser
  • Email Preview Tools: Litmus, Email on Acid for testing across clients
  • Keyboard Navigation: Navigate your email using only Tab, Enter, and arrow keys

Mobile Accessibility Considerations

With over 50% of emails opened on mobile devices, mobile accessibility is critical:

  • Use responsive design that adapts to screen size
  • Ensure font sizes scale appropriately (minimum 16px on mobile)
  • Stack content vertically for easy scrolling
  • Make interactive elements thumb-friendly (minimum 44x44px)
  • Test with device screen readers (VoiceOver, TalkBack)

Remember: Accessibility is an ongoing commitment, not a one-time checklist. Regularly review and update your practices as standards evolve and new assistive technologies emerge.

Conclusion

Creating accessible emails isn't just about compliance—it's about respect and inclusion. By following these best practices, you're ensuring that your message reaches everyone in your audience, regardless of their abilities.

Start with the basics: proper HTML structure, meaningful alt text, sufficient color contrast, and readable typography. Test with real assistive technologies and gather feedback from users with disabilities. Each step you take toward accessibility makes your emails more usable for everyone.

The investment in accessibility pays dividends in increased engagement, broader reach, and a reputation for inclusive, thoughtful communication. In the end, accessible email development is simply good email development.
