The 102KB Limit: Preventing Gmail from Clipping Your Emails

You have just hit "send" on the most important email campaign of the quarter. Your design team crafted a massive, beautiful e-commerce product showcase. The copy is sharp, the imagery is high-resolution, and your team is ready to watch the sales roll in.

But when you check your own Gmail inbox to admire the final product, your heart sinks. You scroll halfway down the email, right before the primary Call-To-Action, and you hit a hard stop. At the bottom of the screen sits a small, grey box with a devastating message:

[Message clipped] View entire message.

This single line of text is the bane of e-commerce brands and marketing agencies everywhere. Not only does it ruin the user experience by hiding your carefully designed content, but the hidden consequences of Gmail clipping can actively destroy your email deliverability, skew your analytics, and drive up your spam complaints.

In this technical guide, I will explain exactly why Gmail does this, the hidden dangers of clipped emails, and the professional development strategies required to keep your HTML code lean and unclipped.

Why Does Gmail Clip Emails?

The explanation is entirely mathematical. Gmail limits the file size of the HTML code it will render in the primary inbox view. If the raw HTML code of your email exceeds exactly 102KB (kilobytes), Gmail will abruptly cut off the rendering and hide the rest of the code behind a link.

It is crucial to understand that this 102KB limit applies only to the actual HTML characters—the text, the <div> tags, the inline CSS, and the URLs. It does not include the file size of the images you link to. You could have ten 2-Megabyte PNG images in your email, and Gmail will not clip it, so long as the actual underlying code remains under 102KB.

Google enforces this limit to ensure that the Gmail web and mobile applications remain lightning-fast and do not consume excess memory or bandwidth when parsing thousands of emails per day.

The Hidden Dangers of a Clipped Email

Most marketers assume that a clipped email is simply an aesthetic annoyance. Unfortunately, the technical reality is much more severe. When Gmail clips your email, it cuts off everything at the bottom of the HTML document. This includes three highly critical elements:

1. The Tracking Pixel is Deleted

Email Service Providers (ESPs) like Mailchimp, Klaviyo, and MailerLite track "Open Rates" by embedding an invisible 1x1 pixel image at the very bottom of your HTML document. When a user opens the email, their email client requests that 1x1 image from the server, and your ESP logs an "Open."

Because the tracking pixel is placed at the bottom, Gmail clipping removes the pixel entirely. If a user opens your clipped email but does not click the "View entire message" link, your ESP will never fire the tracking pixel. Your campaign might have an actual open rate of 45%, but your analytics dashboard will report a dismal 15%.

2. The Unsubscribe Link is Hidden

By law (CAN-SPAM and GDPR), marketing emails must include a clear, accessible unsubscribe link, which is almost universally placed in the email footer. When Gmail clips your email, the footer disappears.

If a frustrated user wants to unsubscribe but cannot find the link, they will take the path of least resistance: They will mark your email as Spam. A spike in spam complaints will severely damage your sender reputation, causing future emails to route directly to the junk folder.

3. Mobile Formatting Breaks

If you hand-code your emails, you likely place your closing </table>, </body>, and </html> tags at the bottom. Gmail's clipping mechanism essentially takes a chainsaw to your document. By severing the HTML before it closes properly, Gmail can inadvertently break the structural integrity of the tables that remain, causing the visible portion of the email to look disjointed and broken on mobile devices.

The Primary Causes of Code Bloat

If your email is hitting the 102KB limit, your code is bloated. Here are the three most common culprits:

1. Drag-and-Drop ESP Builders

As discussed in previous articles, WYSIWYG editors (What You See Is What You Get) generate massive amounts of redundant HTML. Because the builder has to account for every possible user alignment and style choice, a simple text block can generate 30 lines of nested tables. If you build a long e-commerce newsletter using a drag-and-drop builder, you are almost guaranteed to breach 102KB.

2. Base64 Encoded Images

Sometimes, developers try to embed images directly into the HTML using Base64 encoding rather than linking to an externally hosted image URL (e.g., src="https://..."). Base64 converts an image into a massive string of text characters. A single Base64 image can easily add 50KB to 100KB to your HTML file size, instantly triggering the Gmail clip.

3. Leaving Comments in Production Code

When developing custom HTML, developers often leave long HTML comments (<!-- Start Header Section -->) to keep their workspace organized. While useful during development, these characters add up quickly. Leaving developer notes in a production build is a fast way to consume your 102KB budget.

How to Prevent Clipping: The Developer Workflow

To ensure your clients never see a clipped message again, you must implement a strict minification and optimization process before the code is ever loaded into the ESP.

1. Use a Semantic Framework (MJML)

Writing raw HTML from scratch often leads to redundant wrapper tables. Using a modern framework like MJML ensures that the compiled HTML output is as efficient and structurally sound as possible, eliminating unnecessary nesting.

2. HTML Minification

Before delivering the final HTML file to your client or uploading it to the ESP, the code must be minified. This process utilizes a script to strip out every single unnecessary character from the HTML document. It removes:

  • All line breaks (carriage returns)
  • All tabs and indents
  • All HTML comments (except for the conditional `<!--[if mso]>` Ghost Tables required for Outlook)

Minifying a file can routinely reduce the total file size by 15% to 30%, which is often all you need to pull a 115KB file back down to a safe 90KB.

3. Check the File Size Before Sending

Never guess. When your code is finished, save the raw .html file to your desktop. Right-click the file, view its properties, and look at the "Size on disk." If it says 95KB or lower, you are safe (giving the ESP a few extra kilobytes of room to inject their tracking links and headers). If it says 105KB, you need to go back and trim the fat.

Conclusion: Do Less, Link More

If you have minified your custom code and the file is still over 102KB, the harsh reality is that your email is simply too long. An email is not a website; it is an invitation to visit a website.

Instead of putting 20 products in a single email grid, feature your top 4 products and include a clear "Shop the Full Collection" button. By keeping your code lean, your analytics remain accurate, your unsubscribe link stays visible, and your deliverability stays healthy.
