English for Science & Tech Professionals

Engineers, developers, and scientists often have a unique problem: Your technical vocabulary is excellent, but your "connective" language is tricky.

You know the specific terms for your field (algorithms, throughput, thermodynamics), but explaining why those things matter to a non-technical manager can be frustrating. This is often called the "Curse of Knowledge"â€”you know so much that it's hard to explain it simply.

"The goal is not to dumb it down. The goal is to translate it into value."

1. The "Analogy" Technique

When you encounter a blank stare from a client or manager, use an analogy. Connect the complex technical concept to something they already understand.

  • Technical: "The server load is causing high latency due to bandwidth constraints."
  • Analogy: "Imagine a highway with 4 lanes. Suddenly, 10,000 cars try to enter at once. Traffic stops. We need to build more lanes."

2. Bridging the Gap: Tech to Business

The goal of English in a technical role is usually simplification without losing accuracy. You need to focus on the impact, not just the mechanism.

  • Bad: "The latency issues are arising due to the asynchronous callback functions not executing in the correct stack order."
  • Good: "The system is slow because one part of the code is waiting too long for another part to finish. We need to sync them up to improve speed."

3. BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front

In scientific writing, the conclusion often comes last. In business English, the conclusion must come first.

Don't explain your methodology for 10 minutes before giving the result. Start with the result: "The project will be delayed by two days." Then, explain why if they ask.

If you can master the art of "translating" your technical knowledge into simple business English, you become invaluable to your company.
