English for Science & Tech Professionals

Engineers, Developers, and Data Scientists have a unique problem. You know the data better than anyone. You understand the complexity. But when you have to explain it to a non-technical manager (or a client), their eyes glaze over.

The problem usually isn't your English grammar. The problem is that you are speaking "Technical English" to a "Business Audience."

"Your job isn't just to write code or analyze data. Your job is to translate that complexity into business value."

The "What vs. So What" Framework

Technical professionals love to explain WHAT happened. Business leaders want to know SO WHAT (why it matters).

Bad Example (Too Technical):
"The API latency increased by 400ms due to a database lock in the legacy SQL cluster."

Good Example (Business Focused):
"The system is currently slow for users (What). This is happening because of an issue with our old database (Why). We need to migrate to the new server to fix this permanently (So What)."

Simplifying Data for Presentations

When presenting charts or data, avoid reading the numbers. Instead, tell the story of the trend.

  • Instead of: "In Q1 the number was 50, in Q2 it was 60, and in Q3 it is 45."
  • Say: "We saw steady growth in the first half of the year, but we are seeing a slight dip now in Q3. Here is why..."

Softening Your "No"

Technical experts often have to say "No" to impossible requests. But a direct "No" can sound rude or uncooperative in English.

Try these alternatives to sound helpful but firm:

  • "That is a great idea, but technically it would require..."
  • "We can do that, but it will delay the launch by two weeks. Is that a trade-off you are willing to make?"
  • "Instead of feature X, I recommend feature Y because it is faster to build and safer."
